### Navigating Malaysia’s Solar NEM 3.0: Policies, Financial Insights, Maintenance Considerations, and Post-Contract Scenarios

![image](/assets/images/medium/navigating-malaysia-s-solar-nem-3-0-707531224e04-0.png)


As Malaysia moves toward a greener energy future, its **Net Energy Metering (NEM) 3.0** program has become a centerpiece of the country’s push to encourage residential and commercial solar energy adoption. But what exactly are the rules, how long does it last, and what happens once the program ends?

Here’s a comprehensive guide to help you understand everything you need to know if you’re considering investing in solar energy.

---

### What is NEM 3.0?

Launched as a replacement to earlier net metering schemes, **NEM 3.0** allows electricity consumers in Malaysia to install solar photovoltaic (PV) systems to reduce their electricity bills. The program runs until June 30, 2025, or until its quota is fully subscribed.

Under NEM 3.0:

- You **consume the energy you generate** first.
- Any **excess energy exported to the grid** earns you bill credits on a **1-to-1 basis**, offsetting your energy use from Tenaga Nasional Berhad (TNB).
- These credits can be used to **reduce your TNB bill** during lower solar output months (e.g., rainy seasons or night usage).
#### Top Rules Under NEM 3.0

1. **Self-Consumption Comes First:**  
 Solar energy generated is prioritized for on-site use, reducing your real-time electricity usage from the grid.
1. **1:1 Export Offset:**  
 Any excess electricity exported to the grid offsets your energy usage on a kilowatt-hour (kWh) basis.
1. **Valid for 10 Years:**  
 For residential users under the **NEM Rakyat** program, the 1-to-1 offset benefit lasts **10 years** from the date of the agreement.
1. **System Sizing Based on Usage:**

- **Single-phase homes** are limited to **4kW**, and **three-phase homes** up to **10kW**.
- For businesses, solar PV systems cannot exceed **75% of the maximum demand** or **60% of electrical capacity** to maintain grid safety.
#### When Is It Valid?

NEM is valid **all year round**. However, since solar PV only produces energy during daylight hours, the system is most beneficial for users who consume more power during the day (e.g., households with people at home, businesses operating daytime hours).

If your system produces more energy than you consume during the day, the excess is exported to the grid and converted to credit for later use.
#### What Happens After 10 Years?

After the 10-year offset agreement ends:

- **You can still use your solar PV system for self-consumption.**
- **However, any exported energy will no longer earn credits.**
- Your system continues to save money by reducing the amount of energy you draw from the grid.
- It’s important to monitor updates from **SEDA Malaysia** in case new programs replace or extend the offset benefits.
#### Why Sellers Limit System Size

Vendors typically propose system sizes **equal to or slightly below your average monthly electricity bill**. This is not just for financial optimization but also to **comply with government regulations** which restrict over-sizing for safety and grid stability.

### Roof Caution: Water Leakage Risks & Long-Term Maintenance

When installing solar panels on a tile roof, one often overlooked concern is the **risk of water leakage** — which may arise not only from the solar mounting but also from natural roof aging over time.
#### 1. Roof Penetration During Solar Mounting

- Solar panels require mounting brackets that typically penetrate the roof surface.
- Improper installation or inadequate sealing can lead to **moisture seeping** into the roof over time.
- **Panel removal may be needed** for repairs, especially if the leak occurs beneath or around multiple brackets.
#### 2. Sealant Aging — A Common Overlooked Issue

- Even without solar, **roof sealants tend to harden and crack** after 8–12 years due to Malaysia’s tropical heat and UV exposure.
- These cracks may cause leaks **unrelated to your solar installation**.
- This makes it difficult to pinpoint the exact source unless a thorough inspection is done.
#### 3. Prevention Tip: Use Zinc Underlay

To reduce water damage and maintenance costs in the future:

- Install a **zinc or galvanized metal sheet** beneath roof tiles at the solar mounting zones.
- This layer acts as a **secondary waterproof barrier**, catching leaks before they reach your ceiling.
- It’s a one-time effort that can **prevent costly panel removal** years later.

### **Consideration for Battery Storage Integration**

To mitigate the wastage of excess solar energy generated during the day and to further reduce your electricity bills during the post-NEM 3.0 period, integrating a Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) can be beneficial. A BESS allows you to store surplus energy produced during daylight hours for use during nighttime, aligning with your peak consumption period.

​Integrating a Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) into your solar setup can enhance energy self-sufficiency, particularly during nighttime peak consumption. However, it’s crucial to assess the financial implications of such an investment.​
#### Financial Assessment: BESS Investment vs. Projected Savings

- **BESS Investment Cost:** Approximately RM20,000 for a residential system.​
- **Projected Savings Without BESS (Years 7–10):** RM4,320 annually, totaling RM17,280 over four years.​
